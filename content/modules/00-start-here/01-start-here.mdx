## Goal
By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the program is structured, have a weekly study schedule written down, and have a project workspace folder ready on your machine.

## Time + Difficulty
- **Time:** 15–20 min
- **Difficulty:** Beginner

## Why this matters for UX Engineers
- UX Engineers who ship consistently have a repeatable workflow — this program mirrors that rhythm
- Knowing how lessons, practice tasks, and projects connect helps you see your own progress over time
- Setting a schedule up front is the single most reliable predictor of finishing a self-directed course
- Hiring managers look for engineers who can plan their own work and follow through without hand-holding

## What you will produce
- A written 2-sentence learning goal saved in your notes
- A weekly schedule block (days + hours) written in your notes
- A `/projects/sandbox` folder on your machine

## Tools + Where you will work
- **Tools:** Terminal, Cursor, Notes panel (in the app)
- **Folder:** `~/projects/sandbox`
- **Files you'll edit:** Notes (in-app)
- **Commands you'll run:** `mkdir -p ~/projects/sandbox`

## Concept overview
This program is organized into Modules, each covering a skill area like workflow, HTML, CSS, React, or accessibility. Inside each Module are Lessons with step-by-step labs, followed by standalone Practice Tasks and larger Projects. Everything builds toward a Capstone you can put in your portfolio.

Think of Modules as chapters, Lessons as guided labs, and Projects as the portfolio pieces that prove you can apply what you learned.

**How this maps to UX thinking:** Good UX Engineers treat their own learning like a product — they define the goal, timebox the work, and iterate. This orientation lesson is your discovery sprint.

**Mental model:**
- Program structure: Module → Lesson → Practice Task → Project → Capstone
- Each lesson has a single, measurable outcome
- Practice tasks are done without copy-pasting — they test real retention
- Projects are open-ended and portfolio-ready
- The Capstone integrates everything into one shippable piece
- 8–10 hours per week is enough to finish in 12–16 weeks
- Notes are your scratchpad — use them freely

## Step-by-step

### Step 1: Read the program structure
**What:** Open the Curriculum page in this app and read the full module list.
**Where:** Curriculum page (top navigation)
**How:** Click the Curriculum link in the nav. Scroll through all module names and lesson titles.
**Why:** You will make better time estimates and feel less lost mid-lesson when you know the big picture.
**Check:** You can name at least 3 modules and describe what they cover in one sentence each.

### Step 2: Choose your weekly schedule
**What:** Decide which days and hours you will study each week.
**Where:** Your notes panel (in this app) or any note-taking tool you already use
**How:** Write down 3–5 days per week and a 1–3 hour block for each day. Aim for 8–10 hours total per week.
**Why:** A written schedule turns intention into a commitment you can hold yourself to.
**Check:** Your schedule is written down and totals at least 8 hours per week.

### Step 3: Write your 2-sentence learning goal
**What:** Write one sentence on where you are now and one sentence on where you want to be.
**Where:** Notes panel
**How:** Use this format — "Right now I can [current skill level]. By the end of this program I want to be able to [target outcome]."
**Why:** A concrete goal gives every lesson a personal reason to exist.
**Check:** Your goal is specific enough that someone else could tell you in 3 months whether you achieved it.

### Step 4: Create your project workspace
**What:** Create a `/projects/sandbox` folder where all your lesson work will live.
**Where:** Terminal
**How:**
```bash
mkdir -p ~/projects/sandbox
cd ~/projects/sandbox
ls
```
**Why:** Having one predictable folder eliminates the "where did I put that?" problem for the entire course.
**Check:** Running `ls ~/projects` shows a `sandbox` folder.

## If stuck
- **Notes panel not saving:** Try refreshing the page — your notes are persisted in your browser's local storage.
- **mkdir fails with "permission denied":** Run `ls ~` to confirm your home directory is writable, or use `mkdir -p ~/Documents/projects/sandbox` instead.
- **Terminal not opening in Cursor:** Use the menu: View → Terminal, or press Ctrl+` (backtick).

## Common mistakes
- Skipping the Curriculum page and jumping straight to Lesson 1 — you will miss the context that makes each lesson make sense
- Setting an unrealistic schedule (7 days a week, 4 hours a day) that collapses in week 2 — be honest about your actual available time
- Writing a vague learning goal like "get better at code" — a goal you cannot measure cannot guide your decisions
- Not creating the `/projects/sandbox` folder now — later lessons assume it exists
- Using a different folder name than the one specified in each lesson — the paths matter for the steps to work

## Practice task
Without looking back at the lesson, write a 2-sentence learning goal in your notes panel. Then write your weekly schedule — days and hours only, no app names needed. Finally, open a terminal and confirm the sandbox folder exists by running `ls ~/projects`.

- A 2-sentence learning goal is saved in your notes panel
